About

I am a PhD candidate in Mathematical Sciences at Politecnico di Torino, in the Department of Mathematical Sciences (DISMA), supervised by Lamberto Rondoni, and I expect to complete the PhD in November 2026.

From 2025 to 2026 I was a Visiting Student Researcher in Grant Rotskoff's group at Stanford, working on rare event sampling and transition path sampling. The problem is characterizing and sampling transitions between metastable states of a stochastic system when those transitions occur on timescales direct simulation cannot reach, and the tools are large deviation theory, stochastic dynamics, and generative models. This is the direction I want to keep working in, and it draws on the statistical mechanics side of my training with Prof. Rondoni.

Most of my published work is in federated learning, where a model is trained across many clients whose data cannot be pooled and is usually heterogeneous. I developed a method that clusters clients by modelling their interactions through the statistics of their local losses, published at ICML 2025, and methods that identify corrupted clients from compact spectral representations computed once, offline. Sampling arguments turn up here as well, in recent work that selects representative clients by Boltzmann sampling with variance reduction.

Those spectral representations come from the wavelet scattering transform, which I have also applied to marine mammal vocalizations and to glitches in gravitational wave detector data. It carries across all three because it is stable to deformation and invariant to translation whatever the signal happens to be. Separately, through an industrial collaboration with Eltek S.p.A., I developed a physics-informed dimensionality reduction for estimating humidity from sensor time series.

Before the PhD I completed a Master's in Mathematical Engineering at Politecnico di Torino, 110/110 cum laude, with a thesis on the wavelet scattering transform applied to VIRGO gravitational wave data, following a Bachelor's in Mathematics for Engineering at the same institution. My PhD is supported by an industrial scholarship co-funded by the Italian Ministry of University and Research and Eltek S.p.A. I review for NeurIPS and ICLR, and referee for IEEE Journal of Oceanic Engineering and Ecological Informatics.

Research

Federated Learning

Federated learning trains a model across many clients whose data cannot be pooled and are usually heterogeneous. Computation stays decentralized and the data stay on the client, which preserves privacy, and the methods aim to be frugal in communication and computation, keeping them efficient and sustainable as the number of clients grows. My work here clusters clients by modelling their interactions through the statistics of their local losses, and identifies corrupted clients from compact spectral representations computed once, offline.

Marine Mammal Bioacoustics

Classifying marine mammal vocalizations underpins passive acoustic monitoring, where species are identified from underwater recordings rather than by direct sighting. WhaleNet is a deep learning architecture for this problem on the Watkins Marine Mammal Sound Database, combining first and second order wavelet scattering representations with a spectrogram as inputs to the classifier. The wavelet scattering transform used in this work is the shared method across three of these areas.

Gravitational Wave Data Analysis

This work characterizes glitches in gravitational wave detector data. It compares a scattering representation of a glitch with the standard time-frequency (Q-transform) view.

Physics-Informed Machine Learning

Through an industrial collaboration with Eltek S.p.A., this work estimates humidity from sensor time series using a physics-informed dimensionality reduction. A physical model informs the reduction, from which physical parameters are recovered and passed to a network that predicts humidity.
